Monitors under $150: what separates a good one from a frustrating one

Panel type and refresh rate get all the attention, but stand quality and port selection are just as likely to make you regret a budget monitor.

Penny Picks · Updated July 2026 · 10 min read

Budget monitors have improved enormously — full HD IPS panels with 75Hz+ refresh rates are now common well under $150, features that used to require a much bigger budget. But this price tier is also where manufacturers make the most visible tradeoffs: flimsy stands, HDMI-only connectivity, and dim panels are still common ways to hit a low price. Knowing which corners are safe to accept and which aren't will save you from a monitor you regret within a month.

Panel type: IPS for most people, VA for contrast-heavy use

IPS panels are the safer default for most budget shoppers — they hold color and viewing angle consistency well, which matters for everyday work, browsing, and general video. VA panels trade that for deeper contrast and richer blacks, which some people prefer for movies and darker games, but budget VA panels can show noticeable smearing during fast motion. If you're mostly doing office work, documents, or web browsing, IPS is the more forgiving choice.

Refresh rate: 75Hz is a meaningful, affordable upgrade over 60Hz

Even outside of gaming, a 75Hz (or higher) panel noticeably reduces the slight blur and "judder" you get scrolling through documents and web pages on a standard 60Hz screen. It's become common enough in the sub-$150 range that there's little reason to settle for 60Hz unless the rest of the monitor's specs are otherwise clearly superior. True high-refresh gaming (120Hz+) is rarer at this price and usually means accepting a smaller screen or lower resolution as a tradeoff.

Resolution: 1080p is the honest answer under $150

Full HD (1920×1080) is the realistic standard at this price for 24–27 inch screens, and it still looks sharp for productivity and everyday use. Monitors advertised as "4K" under $150 almost always involve compromises elsewhere — a smaller panel, a weaker refresh rate, or a scaler that doesn't handle the resolution smoothly. For most budget shoppers, a well-made 1080p panel beats a corner-cut 4K one.

The stand is easy to overlook and easy to regret

Budget monitors sometimes ship with a stand that only tilts, with no height or swivel adjustment — which can force an uncomfortable neck angle for hours of desk work. Check whether the stand supports height adjustment, and if not, confirm the monitor has 100×100mm VESA mounting holes so you can add an inexpensive monitor arm later.

Ports: check before you assume

Some ultra-budget monitors are HDMI-only, which is fine for a single PC or console but limiting if you want to connect a laptop via DisplayPort or use multiple devices at once. If you plan to run two devices into one monitor, or use it with a laptop that only has USB-C, confirm the exact port selection before buying rather than assuming.

Where the real jump in quality happens

Independent testing tends to agree that monitors right around the $150–200 mark offer noticeably better port selection, brightness, and stand build quality than models several dollars cheaper. If your budget has even a small amount of flexibility, that range is often where a monitor stops feeling like a compromise.

Frequently asked questions

Is HDR worth looking for on a budget monitor?

Generally no. Budget monitors that list "HDR" support usually lack the brightness and contrast range to render it meaningfully — treat it as a checkbox feature rather than a real differentiator at this price.

Is a curved monitor worth it under $150?

It comes down to preference rather than performance. A curve can feel more immersive for gaming or movies at close range, but it doesn't improve image quality itself, and some people find it distracting for document and spreadsheet work.

Should I prioritize screen size or refresh rate?

For most desk setups, a 24-inch panel gives sharper detail per inch than a 27-inch panel at the same 1080p resolution, since the pixels are packed more tightly. If you sit close to your screen, a smaller, sharper panel with a good refresh rate is usually the more satisfying everyday choice than a larger, softer one.
