Power banks: what mAh and watts actually mean for you

The specs on a power bank's box are simple once you know how to translate them into "how many phone charges" and "how fast." Here's the plain-language version.

Penny Picks · Updated July 2026 · 8 min read

Power banks are one of the simplest budget tech purchases once you understand two numbers: capacity (mAh) and output (watts). Everything else — size, build quality, extra ports — is secondary to getting those two right for how you'll actually use it.

mAh tells you capacity — but you never get the full number

A power bank's rated capacity (measured in milliamp-hours) doesn't fully transfer to your device — some energy is lost as heat during conversion. As a rough real-world estimate, expect to get somewhere around two-thirds of the rated capacity as usable charge. That means a 10,000mAh bank realistically delivers roughly one and a half to two full phone charges for most modern smartphones, not exactly "two charges" as the number might suggest.

A simple capacity guide

  • 5,000–6,000mAh: ultralight, pocket-friendly — good for a single top-up, not a full day away from an outlet.
  • 10,000mAh: the everyday sweet spot for most people — covers a full day of moderate phone use with charge to spare.
  • 20,000mAh+: better for travel, multi-device charging, or multi-day trips away from reliable power.

Watts tell you speed — and this is where budget banks often cut corners

Wattage determines how fast a power bank charges your device, independent of its total capacity. A high-capacity bank with low wattage output will still charge your phone slowly — capacity and speed are separate specs, and budget models sometimes lead with an impressive mAh number while quietly shipping weak output. For phone-only charging, look for at least 20W of USB-C Power Delivery (PD) output. If you ever want to charge a laptop from the bank, you'll need significantly more — 65W as a practical minimum, and 100W or more for faster laptop charging.

USB-C PD is the standard worth prioritizing

USB-C Power Delivery has become the common standard across phones, tablets, laptops, and earbuds cases. A power bank without USB-C PD support in 2026 is a real limitation — check for it specifically rather than assuming any USB-C port supports it, since some budget ports are USB-C in shape only, without PD's faster charging negotiation.

Fake capacity claims are a real risk on ultra-cheap, unbranded banks

Extremely cheap, unfamiliar-brand power banks sometimes advertise capacity well beyond what the cells inside can actually deliver. Sticking with an established brand — even a budget line from that brand — is a meaningfully safer bet than an unfamiliar name promising unusually high capacity for an unusually low price. Also look for basic safety certifications (UL, CE, FCC or similar) printed on the product listing; their absence, especially on an unknown brand, is a legitimate red flag rather than a minor detail.

TSA and airline rules, if you're flying with one

Power banks are measured in watt-hours (Wh) for airline purposes, not mAh directly. As a rough conversion, TSA allows up to 100Wh in carry-on luggage without special approval — which works out to roughly 27,000mAh at standard voltage — with 100–160Wh requiring airline approval and anything above that banned outright. Power banks are not allowed in checked luggage under most airline rules, so this only applies to what you carry on. Since exact limits can vary by airline and country, it's worth a quick check with your specific carrier before an international trip.

Frequently asked questions

Is wireless charging on a power bank worth it?

It's a convenience feature, not a performance one — wireless charging is generally slower and less efficient than a cable. It's genuinely useful if you want to skip carrying a cable, but don't expect it to charge as fast as USB-C PD.

Does a built-in cable make a power bank better?

It's a convenience tradeoff, not a clear upgrade — you save the hassle of a separate cable, but you're locked into whatever connector is built in, and a damaged built-in cable is harder to replace than swapping a normal one.

How many charge cycles does a power bank last?

Most lithium-ion power banks are rated for several hundred full charge cycles before capacity noticeably degrades — a couple of years of regular use is a reasonable expectation for a budget model, with better cells and lighter use extending that further.
