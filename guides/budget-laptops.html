The budget laptop buying guide: what actually matters under $500

Two laptops can list nearly identical specs and have completely different lifespans. Here's what separates the ones that hold up from the ones that crawl within a year.

Penny Picks · Updated July 2026 · 12 min read

Sub-$500 laptops have quietly gotten good. A few years ago, "budget laptop" was shorthand for a slow spinning hard drive, 4GB of RAM, and a screen resolution that made text look smudged. That's no longer a given — but it's also not guaranteed just because a laptop is new. The gap between a genuinely usable budget laptop and a frustrating one now comes down to a handful of specific decisions manufacturers make to hit a price point, and most of those decisions are invisible unless you know where to look.

Start with storage type, not storage size

The single spec that predicts whether a budget laptop will feel fast or sluggish isn't the processor name on the sticker — it's whether the storage is an NVMe SSD or the slower eMMC/UFS storage still used to hit lower prices. An SSD boots the machine and opens applications in seconds. eMMC storage is meaningfully slower at everything, and on Windows machines it can struggle to even complete system updates gracefully once the drive starts filling up. If a listing doesn't specify the storage type, that's usually a sign it's eMMC — sellers tend to lead with SSD when they have it.

RAM: 8GB is the realistic floor in 2026

4GB was tolerable for basic browsing a few years ago; it isn't anymore. Modern web pages, browser tabs, and background processes on both Windows and ChromeOS eat memory faster than they used to. Treat 8GB as the minimum for a laptop you intend to use daily, and if you find a model with 16GB near the same price as an 8GB competitor, it's worth the small premium for how much longer the machine will feel comfortable to use.

Chromebook or Windows? It depends on what you actually do

This is the decision that budget shoppers agonize over most, and the honest answer is that it comes down to software, not price.

  • Choose a Chromebook if your work lives in a browser — email, docs, streaming, video calls. ChromeOS runs noticeably smoother than Windows on modest hardware, tends to get several years of security updates, and rarely slows down the way budget Windows machines can over time.
  • Choose Windows if you need specific desktop software — accounting programs, certain creative tools, or anything your school or employer requires. Budget Windows laptops in the $350–500 range are now capable of running Windows 11 without constant frustration, which wasn't reliably true a couple of years ago.

Before you buy a Chromebook

Check the model's Auto Update Expiry (AUE) date — this is the date Google stops sending it security updates. Some Chromebooks are sold well into their support window, meaning a "new" laptop might only have two or three years of updates left. Google publishes a lookup tool for this; search the exact model number before purchasing.

Display: don't accept a resolution from 2013

Look for at least a 1920×1080 (Full HD) panel. Some budget laptops are still sold with 1366×768 displays, which look noticeably soft for reading text and editing documents. It's one of the easiest corners for a manufacturer to cut because it's not the first spec most shoppers check.

Ports and connectivity worth checking

USB-C has become the standard for charging accessories, docking stations, and newer peripherals — a budget laptop without at least one USB-C port is already behind. Beyond that, confirm the laptop has enough full-size USB-A ports for whatever you plan to plug in, since some ultra-thin budget models cut down to just one or two ports total.

The case for buying refurbished

A refurbished business laptop — think the ThinkPad or Latitude lines a couple of generations back — can genuinely outperform a brand-new budget machine at the same price. You often get a metal or magnesium-alloy chassis, a better keyboard, and a faster processor than an equivalent new sub-$500 laptop, because that hardware was originally sold at a much higher price point. The tradeoffs are real: a shorter or nonexistent warranty, unknown battery health, and no guarantee of cosmetic condition. Buy refurbished only from a seller that states a specific battery health percentage and offers at least a 30-day return window.

Frequently asked questions

Is 4GB of RAM ever acceptable in 2026?

Only for extremely light use on a Chromebook — a small number of browser tabs, nothing else running. For a primary device, avoid it. Windows in particular struggles badly with 4GB.

Can I upgrade RAM or storage later on a budget laptop?

Usually not. Most budget laptops solder RAM directly to the motherboard, so buy the amount you'll need up front. Some models do allow a storage upgrade — check the specific model's teardown or user manual before assuming.

Are budget gaming laptops worth it?

Generally no under $500. Meaningful gaming performance requires a dedicated GPU, and laptops that advertise gaming capability at this price point typically pair a weak GPU with cooling that throttles under sustained load. If gaming is the priority, that budget is better spent on a desktop.
