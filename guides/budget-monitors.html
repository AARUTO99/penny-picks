How to choose a budget monitor without regretting it

A good monitor isn't just about refresh rate. Here are the things I would actually check before spending my money.

Penny Picks · Updated July 2026 · 10 min read

Buying a budget monitor is easier than it used to be. Even affordable models now offer Full HD resolution and IPS panels. The problem is that many companies advertise flashy features while cutting corners in places you'll notice every day.

Panel type: IPS for most people, VA for contrast-heavy use

If you're buying a monitor for college, work, or general use, I'd recommend an IPS panel first. The colors look better and the viewing angles are much nicer than most cheap alternatives.

Refresh rate: 75Hz is a meaningful, affordable upgrade over 60Hz

A higher refresh rate is definitely nice, especially if you play games, but don't ignore basic things like brightness and color quality. Those affect your experience every single time you turn the monitor on.

Resolution: 1080p is the honest answer under $150

Take a look at the ports before buying. It sounds boring, but having the right HDMI or DisplayPort connection can save you from buying extra adapters later.

Another thing people often forget is the stand. A shaky stand or one that can't tilt properly becomes annoying surprisingly quickly.

If I had to choose between lots of extra features and better overall build quality, I'd always go for the better-built monitor. It'll probably last longer and be more enjoyable to use.

My advice is to compare a few models, read user reviews, and focus on how you'll actually use the monitor instead of chasing the longest feature list.

Where the real jump in quality happens

Independent testing tends to agree that monitors right around the $150–200 mark offer noticeably better port selection, brightness, and stand build quality than models several dollars cheaper. If your budget has even a small amount of flexibility, that range is often where a monitor stops feeling like a compromise.

Frequently asked questions

Is HDR worth looking for on a budget monitor?

Generally no. Budget monitors that list "HDR" support usually lack the brightness and contrast range to render it meaningfully — treat it as a checkbox feature rather than a real differentiator at this price.

Is a curved monitor worth it under $150?

It comes down to preference rather than performance. A curve can feel more immersive for gaming or movies at close range, but it doesn't improve image quality itself, and some people find it distracting for document and spreadsheet work.

Should I prioritize screen size or refresh rate?

For most desk setups, a 24-inch panel gives sharper detail per inch than a 27-inch panel at the same 1080p resolution, since the pixels are packed more tightly. If you sit close to your screen, a smaller, sharper panel with a good refresh rate is usually the more satisfying everyday choice than a larger, softer one.
